What Happens If You Miss Your 341 Meeting?

The consequences are real -- but they are not always fatal to your case

Life happens. Emergencies, car breakdowns, work conflicts, medical issues -- sometimes you cannot make it to your scheduled 341 meeting. But missing this meeting has real consequences. Here is what happens, what you can do about it, and how to prevent a worst-case outcome.

The Short Answer

Missing your 341 meeting can get your case dismissed.

Attendance at the meeting of creditors is mandatory under 11 U.S.C. Section 343. If you do not appear, the trustee can -- and typically will -- request that your case be dismissed. Dismissal means you lose the protection of the automatic stay, and creditors can resume collection immediately.

What Actually Happens Step by Step

1. The Meeting Is Continued (Rescheduled)

In most districts, if you miss your first 341 meeting, the trustee will continue it -- meaning reschedule it to a later date, usually 2 to 4 weeks out. This is not automatic; it is at the trustee's discretion. Many trustees will continue the meeting once without much pushback, especially if your attorney contacts them in advance to explain the absence.

2. A Motion to Dismiss May Be Filed

If you miss the rescheduled meeting -- or if the trustee decides not to continue it -- the trustee or the U.S. Trustee may file a motion to dismiss your case. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(a) (Chapter 7) or Section 1307(c) (Chapter 13), failure to appear at the meeting of creditors is grounds for dismissal.

3. You Get Notice and a Chance to Respond

If a motion to dismiss is filed, you will receive notice and have an opportunity to respond. This is where your attorney can explain the circumstances and ask the court to keep your case open. Courts are generally willing to give debtors a second chance if the absence was due to a genuine emergency and not a pattern of non-cooperation.

4. The Court Decides

The bankruptcy judge will either grant the dismissal, deny it, or order the meeting rescheduled with conditions. If your case is dismissed, you may be able to refile -- but there are consequences.

Consequences of Dismissal

  • The automatic stay ends. Creditors can resume collection, lawsuits, garnishments, foreclosure, and repossession.
  • You may face a filing bar. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 109(g), if your case is dismissed for willful failure to appear at the 341 meeting, you may be barred from refiling for 180 days.
  • Reduced stay on refiling. Under Section 362(c)(3), if you refile within one year of a dismissal, the automatic stay only lasts 30 days unless you file a motion to extend it.
  • Discharge eligibility issues. If you had a prior bankruptcy case, the timing of dismissal and refiling can affect your eligibility for discharge under Section 1328(f). Use our free screener to check.

What to Do If You Cannot Attend

Before the Meeting

  • Contact your attorney immediately. The sooner they know, the sooner they can request a continuance from the trustee.
  • Provide a reason. Medical emergencies, family emergencies, and work conflicts that truly cannot be moved are generally accepted. "I forgot" is not.
  • Request a continuance in writing. Your attorney should formally request a continuance from the trustee -- ideally before the meeting date.

After You Miss It

  • Call your attorney immediately. Do not wait. Every day of delay makes the situation harder to fix.
  • Document your reason. If you were in the hospital, get a note. If your car broke down, get a receipt from the mechanic. Documentation helps.
  • Be available for the rescheduled date. Missing a second meeting is almost always fatal to your case.

What If Your Attorney Did Not Tell You About the Meeting?

If you missed your 341 meeting because your attorney failed to notify you, failed to send you the notice, or failed to prepare you, that is a serious problem -- but it is a problem with your attorney, not with you. The court may still be willing to reschedule, especially if you can show that the failure was on the attorney's end.

Poor communication from bankruptcy attorneys is unfortunately common. Some firms handle hundreds of cases simultaneously and clients fall through the cracks. If your case was dismissed because your attorney did not do their job, you may have grounds for a malpractice claim or a fee dispute. You can also research your attorney's track record at 1328f.com.

Can the Trustee Refuse to Reschedule?

Yes. Rescheduling is not a right -- it is at the trustee's discretion (and sometimes subject to local court rules). Most trustees will reschedule once. But if you have a history of non-cooperation, failed to file required documents, or your attorney has been unresponsive, the trustee may move directly to dismissal.

Under local rules in many districts, the meeting of creditors must be concluded within a certain timeframe (typically 60 to 120 days after filing). If rescheduling would push the meeting past this deadline, the trustee has less flexibility.

Bottom Line

Do not miss your 341 meeting. It is the single most important appointment in your bankruptcy case. If an emergency makes it impossible to attend, contact your attorney before the meeting -- not after. One missed meeting is usually survivable. Two missed meetings usually is not.
